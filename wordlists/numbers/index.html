Using numbers in context

In Alabama, if we want to talk about how many of something there is, the number goes after the word it describes. This is the opposite of English! So in English, we would say three dogs, but in Alabama, we say ifa totchiina instead!

When we use numbers in a sentence, we put case markers (little pieces that say what role something has in a sentence) instead of the word it follows.

ifa toklok wiikaachiti.

'Two dogs were over there.'

Paspa champoli toklon choopaala

'I will buy two cakes.'

holisso chaffaakan talboolaala.

'I'm going to write one letter.'

Special phrase: "How hold are you?"

In Alabama, we ask someone how old they are by asking Chinnampohchĩ? which literally means "How many are you with?"

. There are two ways to answer this question: Either, by adding the agent suffix -li directly after the dictionary form of the number, or by using the patient prefix cha- before the dictionary form.

Chinnammpohchĩ?

'How old are you?'

Taɬɬaapilihchi.

'I am five years old'

Chataɬɬaapihchi.

'I am five years old'

Numbers greater than 10

Most elders don't use numbers more than ten in Alabama. One way of making multiples of ten that elders do use is 'pokkooli' plus another number, with the prefix ist- at the beginning of it.

Pokkooli istotchiinalihchi.

'I am thirty years old'
